Cajun Pickled Pork – Savoie’s / Richard’s Style

Vinegary, garlicky, mildly spicy, rosy‑red brine, and proper nitrite cure. Preferred cut: pork shoulder for color and texture.

Ingredients (for 1 kg pork shoulder)

  • 1 kg boneless pork shoulder
  • 500 mL distilled white vinegar (5%)
  • 500 mL water
  • 19 g kosher salt
  • 2.5 g Prague Powder #1 (≈ 1/2 level tsp)
  • 5 g sugar
  • 1 Tbsp mustard seed
  • 1/2 tsp celery seed
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 6 garlic cloves, crushed
  • 10–12 black peppercorns
  • 1 tsp Louisiana hot sauce
  • 3/4 tsp Zatarain’s Liquid Crab Boil
  • 1/3 tsp sweet paprika
  • 1/8 tsp mild red chili powder

Method (bag cure, 7 days)

  1. Simmer everything except cure 3–4 min; cool completely.
  2. Stir in Prague Powder #1 once cool.
  3. Vac‑seal pork submerged in brine (or use nonreactive container; press out air).
  4. Refrigerate 7 days at 1–4 °C, turning occasionally.
  5. Rinse lightly. Cured but raw.

Use: Cube/slice; simmer in beans/greens/cabbage until tender. Storage: 10–14 d fridge from start; 3–4 mo frozen.

Safety: Keep cold. Don’t exceed cure. Never add cure to hot liquid.

Tarte à la Bouille (Cajun Custard) Pie

South Louisiana classic: a silky stovetop custard (pastry‑cream style) poured into a fully baked pie shell and finished with nutmeg (or a light meringue). Not overly sweet; clean vanilla‑milk flavor.

Crust

Use one fully baked 9" (23 cm) pie shell. You can reuse the All‑Butter Pie Crust above — blind bake it completely to golden before filling.

Custard filling

  • Dairy (3 cups / 720 ml total): 2 cups (480 ml) whole milk + 1 cup (240 ml) evaporated milk
  • Sugar: 150 g (about 3/4 cup)
  • Flour: 40 g (1/3 cup) all‑purpose
  • Cornstarch: 16 g (2 Tbsp)
  • Egg yolks: 4 large (or 3 whole eggs, but yolks give silkier texture)
  • Unsalted butter: 28 g (2 Tbsp)
  • Vanilla: 1 1/2 tsp extract (or 1 tsp extract + 1/2 vanilla bean scraped)
  • Salt: small pinch
  • Finish: freshly grated nutmeg (and a pinch of cinnamon, optional)

Method

  1. Pre‑bake crust: Bake crust fully to golden and let it cool. Keep the shell ready on a tray.
  2. Dry mix: In a heavy saucepan off heat, whisk sugar, flour, cornstarch, salt until no lumps remain.
  3. Add dairy: Whisk in the milk + evaporated milk gradually until smooth.
  4. Cook custard: Set over medium heat, whisking constantly. When it thickens and bubbles, cook 2–3 minutes more to cook out starch.
  5. Temper eggs: In a bowl, whisk yolks. Slowly stream in 1 cup of the hot custard while whisking. Return egg mixture to the pot; cook 1–2 minutes more, whisking, to 180–185 °F / 82–85 °C. Remove from heat.
  6. Finish: Whisk in butter and vanilla until glossy. Strain through a fine sieve for extra silky texture.
  7. Fill: Immediately pour hot custard into the baked shell; smooth the top. Dust lightly with nutmeg (and a touch of cinnamon if desired).
  8. Set: Cool to room temp, then refrigerate 3–4 hours until sliceable.

Optional meringue finish (old‑school)

If you like a meringue top: keep custard hot in the shell, whip 3 egg whites with 1/4 tsp cream of tartar to soft peaks, then rain in 6 Tbsp sugar to stiff‑glossy. Spread over hot custard, sealing edges. Bake 10–12 min @ 350 °F / 175 °C until tips are golden. Cool completely.

Notes & scaling

  • Sweeter? Increase sugar to 170–180 g (about 7/8 cup).
  • Softer set? Reduce cornstarch by 1 tsp; firmer set → add 1 tsp more.
  • Flavor twists: Lemon: add 1–2 tsp lemon zest with dairy. Coconut: swap 1 cup milk for coconut milk.
  • Scale: For a deep‑dish pie, multiply custard amounts by 1.25×.
  • Storage: Refrigerate; best within 3 days. Do not freeze (custard weeps).

Louisiana Pantry Notes

  • Trinity (onion:celery:green bell) = 2:1:1 by volume.
  • Dark roux = equal parts fat/flour by weight; patience over high heat.
  • Add garlic late to avoid burning.
  • Finish with a splash of acid (lemon, cane, or cider vinegar).

Philippine Substitutions

  • Andouille → smoked longganisa + more black pepper & paprika.
  • Celery ribs → kinchay stems/leaves (use less; stronger).
  • US bay → local laurel (usually stronger; use less).
  • Buttermilk → milk + 1 Tbsp lemon/calamansi per cup, 5–10 min.
  • Creole mustard → Dijon + a little vinegar + pinch cayenne.

Base Blends & Pastes

House Cajun seasoning (base)

  • 3 Tbsp paprika • 1 Tbsp each garlic & onion powder • 1 Tbsp fine salt
  • 2 tsp black pepper • 1–2 tsp cayenne • 1 tsp each thyme & oregano

Creole butter injection (base)

  • 250 mL melted butter (or 50/50 with neutral oil)
  • 2–3 tsp House Cajun seasoning • 1–2 tsp lemon or cane vinegar

Shopping + Ingredient Notes / Local Hacks

  • Prague Powder #1: use only at recommended rate; never add to hot liquid.
  • Tiny amounts of Zatarain’s liquid crab boil add the boil‑spice vibe.
  • Many Cajun blends are salty; adjust added salt accordingly.
  • Roast kalabasa instead of boiling to avoid watery puree.
