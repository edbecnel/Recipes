Cajun Pickled Pork – Savoie’s / Richard’s Style

This version tracks the classic Louisiana commercial flavor/texture (Savoie’s, Richard’s): vinegary, garlicky, mildly spicy, rosy‑red brine, and proper nitrite cure. It uses pork shoulder and adds a touch of paprika/chili for the characteristic color.

Preferred cut

Use boneless pork shoulder (pork butt). The fat and myoglobin give the right rosy color and tender bite after curing. Lean cuts like loin cure but stay paler/firm.

Ingredients (for 1 kg pork shoulder)

  • 1 kg boneless pork shoulder (whole or cut into large ~4 cm chunks)
  • 500 mL distilled white vinegar (5%)
  • 500 mL water
  • 19 g kosher salt (≈ 3 tsp)
  • 2.5 g Prague Powder #1 (pink curing salt #1, 6.25% nitrite) — ≈ 1/2 tsp level (Do not exceed this amount.)
  • 5 g sugar (≈ 1 tsp)
  • 1 Tbsp mustard seed
  • 1/2 tsp celery seed
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 6 garlic cloves, crushed
  • 10–12 black peppercorns
  • 1 tsp Louisiana hot sauce (Crystal/Louisiana brand)
  • 3/4 tsp Zatarain’s Liquid Crab Boil
  • 1/3 tsp sweet paprika
  • 1/8 tsp mild red chili powder (not cayenne)

Method (bag cure, 7 days)

  1. Make spiced brine (no cure yet): In a non‑reactive pot, combine vinegar, water, kosher salt, sugar, mustard seed, celery seed, bay leaves, garlic, peppercorns, hot sauce, Zatarain’s, paprika, and chili powder. Bring to a boil; simmer 3–4 min to bloom spices. Remove from heat and cool completely to room temp.
  2. Add cure cold: When the brine is fully cool, stir in Prague Powder #1 until dissolved. Never add curing salt to hot liquid.
  3. Bag & seal: Place pork in a heavy vacuum bag; pour in brine to fully submerge. Vacuum on “moist/gentle” if available and seal. (If no sealer, use a glass/stainless container and press out air.)
  4. Cure cold: Refrigerate 1–4 °C / 34–40 °F for 7 days, turning the bag every day or two to ensure even exposure. (Total 7–10 days is fine for large pieces.)
  5. After curing: Remove pork from brine. Rinse lightly to remove loose seeds/spices. The meat is cured but still raw.

To cook / use

Slice, cube, or leave in slabs. Use like classic pickle meat: add directly to pots of red beans, white beans, greens, cabbage, etc., and simmer until fully cooked tender. The brine is salty—season the pot to taste.

Vacuum sealing tips

Vacuum minimizes oxygen and helps maintain the deep red hue. Seal flat in a single layer so the brine contacts all surfaces; rotate during cure.

Storage after cure

  • Short‑term (fridge): keep sealed with brine at 1–3 °C / 34–38 °F and use within 10–14 days total from start of cure.
  • Long‑term (freeze): freeze in brine (in the bag) or pat dry and re‑seal; use within 3–4 months for best quality. Thaw in the refrigerator and cook within 3–5 days.
Safety: Always keep refrigerated; do not exceed Prague Powder #1 amount; never add cure to hot liquid.

Cajun Butter‑Injected Roasted Turkey

Goal: juicy dark + white meat, buttery/garlicky Creole interior, Cajun‑spiced skin.

1) Injection marinade (for ~12–20 lb / 5.5–9 kg turkey)

Blend smooth, then strain so it won’t clog the injector:

  • 1 cup (240 ml) unsalted butter, melted
  • 1 cup (240 ml) low‑sodium chicken stock
  • 2 Tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 2 Tbsp hot sauce
  • 1 Tbsp Cajun/Creole seasoning
  • 1 Tbsp garlic powder
  • 1 tsp onion powder
  • 1 tsp paprika

Where to inject: both sides of the breast (multiple angles), thighs, drumsticks. Aim for several small pockets rather than one big pool.

2) Cajun dry rub / outside paste

  • 3 Tbsp Cajun seasoning
  • 1 Tbsp kosher salt (skip if your Cajun blend is very salty)
  • 2 tsp smoked paprika
  • 2 tsp garlic powder
  • 1 tsp dried thyme
  • 1 tsp dried oregano
  • Black pepper to taste

Massage the bird: pat very dry; rub a thin film of softened butter or oil under the breast skin and all over; coat surface (and under loose breast skin) with the dry rub. Air‑dry uncovered in the fridge overnight for crisp skin.

3) Roast day game plan

  • Take turkey out of fridge 30–40 min before roasting.
  • Preheat oven to 325 °F / 163 °C.
  • Rack + pan, breast up. Tuck wingtips.

Roast at 325 °F until breast ~150 °F / 66 °C and thigh ~165–168 °F / 74–75 °C. Then crank to 400 °F / 204 °C to brown. Pull when breast ~160 °F / 71 °C and thigh/drum ~175 °F / 79 °C. Rest loosely tented 20–30 min.

4) Approximate cook timing (thermometer beats clock)

  • 12 lb (5.5 kg): ~2 hr 35 min (156 min)
  • 16 lb (7.25 kg): ~3 hr 30 min (208 min)
  • 20 lb (9 kg): ~4 hr 20 min (260 min)
  • 25 lb (11.3 kg): ~5 hr 25 min (325 min)

Stuffing the cavity slows and is not recommended. Cook dressing on the side.

Kalabasa “Pumpkin” Pie + Buttery Crust

Using local kalabasa squash instead of canned pumpkin. Roast/steam first, mash, and drain off extra water so it’s thick.

A) All‑butter pie crust (1 × 9" / 23 cm shell)

Dry mix: 200 g AP flour (~1 2/3 cups) • 2 tsp sugar • 1/2 tsp fine salt
Fat: 115 g very cold unsalted butter (8 Tbsp / 1 stick)
Liquid: 2–4 Tbsp ice water • Optional 1 tsp vinegar or vodka

Method: Chill bowl, flour, butter → cut butter to pea‑size → sprinkle water just to bind → wrap + chill ≥1 hr → roll, pan, crimp → chill 20+ min → blind bake 10–12 min @ 350 °F / 175 °C with weights.

B) Kalabasa custard filling (for one 9" pie)

  • 2 cups (~480 g) roasted, well‑drained kalabasa puree
  • 2 large eggs + 1 extra yolk
  • 150 g packed brown sugar (~3/4 cup) + 2 Tbsp white sugar
  • 1 cup (240 ml) evaporated milk (or 1/2 cup evap + 1/2 cup coconut milk)
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 1 1/2 tsp cinnamon • 1/2 tsp ginger • 1/4 tsp nutmeg • 1/8 tsp clove • pinch salt

Method: Whisk eggs/sugars/spices/salt → whisk in kalabasa + vanilla → stream in milk → pour into warm, par‑baked crust → bake 50–60 min @ 350 °F / 175 °C until edges set and center wobbles. Target internal 175–180 °F / 79–82 °C. Cool ≥3 hr.

C) Mini tart shells (50 ml each)

Shell steals volume; plan ~35–40 ml custard each. Blind‑bake briefly → fill → bake 20–25 min @ 350 °F / 175 °C until just set.

D) Flavor upgrades / troubleshooting

  • Bland dough → ensure salt + a little sugar; bake crust to light golden. Cold dough = flake + flavor.
  • Not flaky → visible butter pieces + cold everything.

Cost estimate (PH, rough): Butter ₱50–60 (~115 g) • Flour ₱10 • Eggs ₱20 (for two) • Evap ₱35 • Sugar/spices ₱20–30 • Kalabasa ~family price. Total ~₱150–200+ depending on brands.

Crispy Lemon–Chili Beef Shank (Working)

Shredded, pan‑crisped beef shank with a spicy‑tangy braise made from lemon and dried Asian/Korean/Chinese chilies. Not a stew: braise → shred → reduce liquid → pan‑crisp until sticky‑crispy edges.

Yield (base): ~1.5–2 lb (0.7–1 kg) beef shank • Method: oven/stovetop low braise + pan crisp
Style: Louisiana‑meets‑Asian dried chili

Ingredients

For the beef
~1.5–2 lb (0.7–1 kg) beef shank, bone‑in or thick cross‑cut • Salt & black pepper • ~1 Tbsp neutral oil (for browning)

Braise sauce (to blend)

Chilies — see options

Chili options (choose one):

  • Option 1 — Asian dried chilies: 3–5 dried red chilies, mostly deseeded (if very small/hot, start with 2–3)
  • Option 2 — Ancho/guajillo: 1 dried ancho + 1 dried guajillo (or 2× ancho)
  • 1/2 cup (120 ml) beef stock or water
  • 1 Tbsp tomato paste
  • 1 Tbsp vinegar (apple cider, cane, or rice)
  • Juice of ~1/2 lemon (~10–15 ml); reserve the rest
  • 1/2 small onion, roughly chopped
  • 3 cloves garlic
  • 1 tsp dried oregano
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1/2 tsp smoked paprika (or regular)
  • 1/4 tsp ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 tsp soy sauce (optional, umami boost)
  • 1 bay leaf (do not blend; add whole later)
  • 1 tsp salt (to start) • ~1/2 tsp black pepper

Method

  1. Prep chilies: Toast lightly 20–30 s/side in a dry pan until fragrant; soak in hot water 10–15 min and drain. (If using already‑soft Korean chilies, brief soak or skip.)
  2. Blend sauce: Chilies + all blender items except bay leaf → perfectly smooth. Taste bold, a bit salty/tangy/spicy. Brighten with a few drops more lemon only if flat.
  3. Brown beef: Pat dry, season, and sear hard in neutral oil until deeply browned.
  4. Braise: Pour in blended sauce; add bay leaf. Liquid should come ≥ halfway up meat (add splash stock as needed). Cover and cook 300°F / 150°C (oven) or gentle simmer ~3–3.5 h, flipping once or twice, until fork‑twist tender.
  5. Shred + reduce: Remove meat; discard bones/gristle. Shred or chunk. Reduce braising liquid uncovered over medium heat to a sauce that coats a spoon; salt to taste.
  6. Crisp: Hot skillet, single layer of meat. Spoon a little reduced sauce over. Let it sit to caramelize; flip once to crisp second side.

Serve: Tortillas w/ onion + cilantro/scallions; over rice w/ cucumber + squeeze of lemon; on garlic fried rice w/ fried egg; or in a baked potato w/ cheese.

Scaling notes: Base batch uses ~0.7–1.0 kg shank. For half‑batch, use ~0.35–0.5 kg shank and multiply all ingredients by 0.5.

Sticky Soy‑Ginger Star Anise Beef Shank (Working)

Deep, glossy, savory‑sweet braised beef shank; reduce the liquid to a shiny lacquered sauce.

Yield (base): ~1.5–2 lb (0.7–1 kg) beef shank • Method: low braise → reduce to glaze
Style: Soy‑ginger with warm spice

Ingredients

  • ~1.5–2 lb (0.7–1 kg) beef shank, bone‑in or thick cross‑cut
  • ~1 Tbsp neutral oil (for browning)
  • ≈40 ml (about 2 1/2 Tbsp) soy sauce
  • 1 Tbsp dark soy (optional; else +1 Tbsp regular soy + 1/2 tsp molasses/brown sugar)
  • 1 1/2 Tbsp brown or palm sugar (packed)
  • 1/2 Tbsp honey (~1 1/2 tsp)
  • 2 Tbsp (30 ml) rice vinegar or apple cider vinegar
  • ~1/2 thumb fresh ginger, sliced
  • 3 cloves garlic, smashed
  • 1 whole star anise
  • 1/2 small cinnamon stick (or a pinch of ground cinnamon)
  • 1/2 tsp coarsely cracked black pepper
  • 1 cup (240 ml) water or beef stock
  • 1 scallion in 2‑inch chunks (optional)

Method

  1. Brown beef: Pat dry, season lightly; sear well on all sides in neutral oil until deep brown.
  2. Build braise liquid: Add ginger, garlic, star anise, cinnamon, and cracked pepper to pot; cook ~30 s. Pour in soy, dark soy (if using), brown sugar, honey, vinegar, and water/stock; scrape up fond. Add scallion chunks. Liquid should come halfway up meat.
  3. Slow braise: Cover; gentle stovetop simmer on low or 300°F / 150°C oven 2.5–3 h, flipping halfway, until fork‑twist tender.
  4. Reduce to glaze: Remove shank; boil liquid uncovered over medium‑high until thick and glossy. If too salty, add a splash of water and simmer 1 min.
  5. Slice & finish: Slice/chunk across the grain; spoon glossy sauce over. Garnish with scallions/sesame if desired.

Leftovers: Shred and pan‑fry until crisp; glaze with a spoon of sauce; stuff into steamed buns or tortillas.

Scaling notes: For half‑batch, use ~0.35–0.5 kg shank and multiply all ingredients by 0.5.

Scaling & Conversions

Scale factor = Desired yield ÷ Base yield.
Example: Base 2.0 kg → want 1.0 kg ⇒ factor = 0.5 (halve all amounts).

  • Rounding: Salt/spices: nearest 0.1 g (or ⅛ tsp). Herbs/peppers: round to taste; note changes. Liquids: 1 mL under 100 mL; else 5 mL.

Common conversions

  • 1 tsp = 5 mL • 1 Tbsp = 15 mL • 1 cup (US) ≈ 240 mL
  • 1 oz = 28.35 g • 1 lb = 454 g
  • 350°F ≈ 177°C • 400°F ≈ 204°C • 425°F ≈ 218°C

Handy densities (approx.)

  • Fine salt: 1 tsp ≈ 6 g
  • Granulated sugar: 1 cup ≈ 200 g
  • All‑purpose flour: 1 cup ≈ 120 g

Louisiana Pantry Notes

  • Cajun/Creole “trinity”: onion : celery : green bell pepper = 2 : 1 : 1 (by volume).
  • Dark roux for gumbo/gravies: equal parts oil/butter & flour by weight. Patience > high heat.
  • Holy trinity + garlic: add minced garlic late so it doesn’t burn.
  • Acid for balance: lemon, cane vinegar, or cider vinegar splash at finish.

Philippine Substitutions

  • Andouille → local smoked longganisa or garlicky pork sausage; add extra black pepper + paprika.
  • Celery ribs → kinchay stems/leaves; for strong celery note, use celery salt carefully.
  • Bay leaves (US) → local laurel leaves (usually stronger; use less).
  • Buttermilk → milk + 1 Tbsp lemon/calamansi per cup, rest 5–10 min.
  • Creole mustard → Dijon + a little vinegar + pinch cayenne.
  • Worcestershire → local brands OK; adjust salt.

Shopping + Ingredient Notes / Local Hacks

  • Prague Powder #1 (a.k.a. InstaCure #1 / pink curing salt #1): Use only at the recommended rate. It is not table salt; it’s dyed pink to avoid confusion.
  • Zatarain’s liquid crab boil: Tiny amounts add that south Louisiana boiled‑seafood vibe to pickled pork brine without making it taste like straight crab boil.
  • Tony Chachere’s / Slap Ya Mama and similar: Many are salty. When a recipe also calls for kosher salt, taste first.
  • Local kalabasa: Roast instead of boiling to drive off water and concentrate sweetness. Watery puree → bland pie.
