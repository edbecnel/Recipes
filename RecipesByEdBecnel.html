Cajun Pickled Pork – Savoie’s / Richard’s Style

This tracks the classic Louisiana commercial flavor/texture (Savoie’s, Richard’s): vinegary, garlicky, mildly spicy, rosy-red brine, and proper nitrite cure. Uses pork shoulder and a touch of paprika/chili for the color.

Preferred cut

Boneless pork shoulder (pork butt). Fat + myoglobin = rosy color and tender bite after curing. Loin will cure but stays paler/firm.

Ingredients (for 1 kg pork shoulder)

  • 1 kg boneless pork shoulder (whole or ~4 cm chunks)
  • 500 mL distilled white vinegar (5%)
  • 500 mL water
  • 19 g kosher salt (~3 tsp)
  • 2.5 g Prague Powder #1 (pink curing salt #1, 6.25% nitrite) — about 1/2 tsp level. Do not exceed this amount.
  • 5 g sugar (~1 tsp)
  • 1 Tbsp mustard seed
  • 1/2 tsp celery seed
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 6 garlic cloves, crushed
  • 10–12 black peppercorns
  • 1 tsp Louisiana hot sauce
  • 3/4 tsp Zatarain’s Liquid Crab Boil
  • 1/3 tsp sweet paprika
  • 1/8 tsp mild red chili powder (not cayenne)

Method (bag cure, 7 days)

  1. Make spiced brine (no cure yet): combine vinegar, water, kosher salt, sugar, mustard seed, celery seed, bay leaves, garlic, peppercorns, hot sauce, Zatarain’s, paprika, and chili. Boil, then simmer 3–4 min. Cool completely.
  2. Add cure cold: stir in Prague Powder #1 when the brine is fully cool. Never add curing salt to hot liquid.
  3. Bag & seal: Pork into a heavy vacuum bag; pour in brine to submerge. Vacuum-seal if possible. (If no sealer, pack tightly in glass/stainless.)
  4. Cure cold: Refrigerate 34–40 °F / 1–4 °C for ~7 days, flipping/turning daily.
  5. After curing: Remove pork, rinse lightly to remove loose spices. Meat is cured but still raw.

To cook / use

Slice or cube and add straight to pots of red beans, white beans, greens, cabbage, etc. Simmer until tender. Brine is salty — taste before salting the pot.

Vacuum sealing tips

Vacuum = less oxygen, deeper red color. Seal flat in a single layer so brine touches all surfaces; rotate the bag through the week.

Storage after cure

  • Fridge (short-term): keep sealed in brine at 34–38 °F / 1–3 °C. Use within 10–14 days total from start of cure.
  • Freeze (long-term): freeze in brine or pat dry + reseal. Best within ~3–4 months. Thaw in fridge and cook within 3–5 days.

Food safety: Always keep refrigerated. Do not exceed the Prague Powder #1 amount. Never add cure to hot liquid.

Cajun Butter-Injected Roasted Turkey

Goal: juicy dark + white meat, buttery/garlicky Creole interior, Cajun-spiced skin.

1) Injection marinade (12–20 lb / 5.5–9 kg turkey)

Blend smooth, then strain so it won’t clog the injector:

  • 1 cup (240 mL) unsalted butter, melted
  • 1 cup (240 mL) low-sodium chicken stock
  • 2 Tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 2 Tbsp hot sauce
  • 1 Tbsp Cajun/Creole seasoning
  • 1 Tbsp garlic powder
  • 1 tsp onion powder
  • 1 tsp paprika

Where to inject: both sides of the breast (multiple angles), thighs, drumsticks. Many small pockets > one big pool.

2) Cajun dry rub / outside paste

  • 3 Tbsp Cajun seasoning
  • 1 Tbsp kosher salt (skip if your Cajun blend is very salty)
  • 2 tsp smoked paprika
  • 2 tsp garlic powder
  • 1 tsp dried thyme
  • 1 tsp dried oregano
  • Black pepper to taste

Pat bird very dry. Rub a thin film of butter or oil under the breast skin and all over. Coat with the dry rub (get some under the loose breast skin). Air-dry overnight, uncovered in the fridge, for crisp skin.

3) Roast day game plan

  • Take turkey out of fridge ~30–40 min before roasting.
  • Preheat oven to 325 °F / 163 °C.
  • Rack + pan, breast up. Tuck wingtips.

Roast at 325 °F until breast ~150 °F / 66 °C and thigh ~165–168 °F / 74–75 °C. Then crank to 400 °F / 204 °C to brown. Pull when breast ~160 °F / 71 °C and thigh/drum ~175 °F / 79 °C. Rest loosely tented 20–30 min.

4) Approximate cook timing (thermometer beats clock)

  • 12 lb (5.5 kg): ~2 hr 35 min (~156 min)
  • 16 lb (7.25 kg): ~3 hr 30 min (~208 min)
  • 20 lb (9 kg): ~4 hr 20 min (~260 min)
  • 25 lb (11.3 kg): ~5 hr 25 min (~325 min)

Note: Stuffing slows things down. Cook dressing on the side.

Kalabasa “Pumpkin” Pie + Buttery Crust

Local kalabasa squash instead of canned pumpkin. Roast or steam first, mash, and drain so it’s thick.

A) All-butter pie crust (1 × 9" / 23 cm shell)

Dry mix: 200 g AP flour (~1 2/3 cups) • 2 tsp sugar • 1/2 tsp fine salt
Fat: 115 g very cold unsalted butter (8 Tbsp / 1 stick)
Liquid: 2–4 Tbsp ice water • Optional 1 tsp vinegar or vodka

Chill bowl, flour, butter → cut butter to pea size → sprinkle water just to bind → wrap & chill ≥1 hr → roll, pan, crimp → chill 20+ min → blind bake 10–12 min @ 350 °F / 175 °C with weights.

B) Kalabasa custard filling (for one 9" pie)

  • 2 cups (~480 g) roasted, well-drained kalabasa puree
  • 2 large eggs + 1 extra yolk
  • 150 g packed brown sugar (~3/4 cup) + 2 Tbsp white sugar
  • 1 cup (240 mL) evaporated milk (or half evap / half coconut milk)
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 1 1/2 tsp cinnamon • 1/2 tsp ginger • 1/4 tsp nutmeg • 1/8 tsp clove • pinch salt

Whisk eggs/sugars/spices/salt → whisk in kalabasa + vanilla → stream in milk → pour into warm, par-baked crust → bake 50–60 min @ 350 °F / 175 °C until edges set and center just wobbles. Target internal 175–180 °F / 79–82 °C. Cool ≥3 hr.

C) Mini tart shells (50 mL each)

Shell steals volume; plan ~35–40 mL custard each. Blind-bake briefly → fill → bake 20–25 min @ 350 °F / 175 °C until just set.

D) Flavor fixes

  • Bland dough → add salt + a pinch of sugar; bake crust to light golden. Cold dough = flaky dough.
  • Not flaky → you need visible butter pieces and cold everything.

Rough PH cost: butter ₱50–60 (~115 g) • flour ₱10 • eggs ₱20 (2 pcs) • evap ₱35 • sugar/spices ₱20–30 • kalabasa (family price). Total ~₱150–200+.

Crispy Lemon–Chili Beef Shank (Working)

Shredded, pan-crisped beef shank with a spicy-tangy braise from lemon and dried chilies. Not a stew: braise → shred → reduce liquid → pan-crisp until sticky-crispy.

Yield (base): ~1.5–2 lb (0.7–1 kg) beef shank
Method: oven/stovetop low braise + pan crisp
Style: Louisiana-meets-Asian dried chili

Braise sauce (to blend)

Choose one chili path:

  • Asian dried chilies: 3–5 dried red chilies, mostly deseeded (less if tiny/hot)
  • Ancho/guajillo: 1 dried ancho + 1 dried guajillo (or 2× ancho)
  • 1/2 cup (120 mL) beef stock or water
  • 1 Tbsp tomato paste
  • 1 Tbsp vinegar (apple cider, cane, or rice)
  • Juice of ~1/2 lemon (~10–15 mL), reserve rest
  • 1/2 small onion, roughly chopped
  • 3 cloves garlic
  • 1 tsp dried oregano
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1/2 tsp smoked paprika
  • 1/4 tsp ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 tsp soy sauce (optional)
  • 1 bay leaf (don’t blend; add whole later)
  • 1 tsp salt to start • ~1/2 tsp black pepper

Method

  1. Toast chilies briefly & soak 10–15 min. Blend everything (except bay leaf) perfectly smooth. Bright, salty/tangy/spicy.
  2. Brown seasoned beef shank hard in a little neutral oil.
  3. Pour in blended sauce + bay leaf. Liquid should come at least halfway up the meat (add splash stock if needed). Cover and cook ~300 °F / 150 °C or gentle simmer ~3–3.5 h, flipping once or twice, until fork-twist tender.
  4. Shred. Reduce braising liquid uncovered to a thick sauce. Salt to taste.
  5. Crisp shreds in a hot skillet in a single layer, spooning a bit of reduced sauce over. Let it caramelize, flip once, get sticky-crispy edges.

Serve in tortillas with onion + cilantro/scallions, over rice with cucumber + lemon, over garlic fried rice with fried egg, or stuffed in a baked potato with cheese.

Sticky Soy-Ginger Star Anise Beef Shank (Working)

Deep, glossy, savory-sweet braise finished as a shiny lacquered sauce.

Yield (base): ~1.5–2 lb (0.7–1 kg) beef shank
Method: low braise → reduce to glaze
Style: Soy-ginger with warm spice

Ingredients

  • ~1.5–2 lb (0.7–1 kg) beef shank, bone-in or thick cross-cut
  • ~1 Tbsp neutral oil
  • ≈40 mL (~2 1/2 Tbsp) soy sauce
  • 1 Tbsp dark soy (or +1 Tbsp regular soy + 1/2 tsp molasses/brown sugar)
  • 1 1/2 Tbsp brown or palm sugar (packed)
  • 1/2 Tbsp honey (~1 1/2 tsp)
  • 2 Tbsp (30 mL) rice vinegar or apple cider vinegar
  • ~1/2 thumb fresh ginger, sliced
  • 3 cloves garlic, smashed
  • 1 whole star anise
  • 1/2 small cinnamon stick (or pinch ground cinnamon)
  • 1/2 tsp coarsely cracked black pepper
  • 1 cup (240 mL) water or beef stock
  • 1 scallion in 2-inch chunks (optional)

Method

  1. Brown beef shank well on all sides.
  2. Add ginger, garlic, star anise, cinnamon, pepper. Splash in soy sauces, brown sugar, honey, vinegar, and water/stock; scrape up fond. Add scallion.
  3. Cover and braise gently on low heat or 300 °F / 150 °C oven 2.5–3 h, flipping halfway, until fork-twist tender.
  4. Pull meat out. Boil liquid uncovered until thick and glossy. If it gets too salty, add a splash of water and simmer 1 min.
  5. Slice/chunk meat across the grain and spoon glaze over. Leftovers shred and pan-fry great — stuff into buns, tortillas, fried rice, etc.

Scaling & Conversions

Scale factor = Desired yield ÷ Base yield.
Example: Base 2.0 kg → want 1.0 kg ⇒ factor = 0.5 (halve all amounts).

  • Salt/spices: round to nearest 0.1 g (or 1/8 tsp).
  • Herbs/peppers: round to taste; note changes.
  • Liquids: 1 mL steps under 100 mL; else 5 mL steps.

Common conversions

  • 1 tsp = 5 mL
  • 1 Tbsp = 15 mL
  • 1 cup (US) ≈ 240 mL
  • 1 oz = 28.35 g
  • 1 lb = 454 g
  • 350 °F ≈ 177 °C
  • 400 °F ≈ 204 °C
  • 425 °F ≈ 218 °C

Handy densities (approx.)

  • Fine salt: 1 tsp ≈ 6 g
  • Granulated sugar: 1 cup ≈ 200 g
  • All-purpose flour: 1 cup ≈ 120 g

Louisiana Pantry Notes

  • Cajun/Creole “trinity”: onion : celery : green bell pepper = 2 : 1 : 1 (by volume).
  • Dark roux for gumbo/gravies: equal parts oil/butter & flour by weight. Low/steady heat, patience.
  • “Holy trinity + garlic”: add garlic late so it doesn’t burn.
  • Finish with acid: lemon, cane vinegar, or cider vinegar splash to balance richness.

Philippine Substitutions

  • Andouille → local smoked longganisa / garlicky pork sausage; add extra black pepper + paprika.
  • Celery ribs → kinchay stems/leaves; for strong celery note, tiny bit of celery salt.
  • Bay leaves (US) → local laurel leaves (usually stronger; use less).
  • Buttermilk → milk + 1 Tbsp lemon/calamansi per cup, rest 5–10 min.
  • Creole mustard → Dijon + a little vinegar + pinch cayenne.
  • Worcestershire → local brands are fine; adjust salt.

Shopping + Ingredient Notes / Local Hacks

  • Prague Powder #1 (InstaCure #1 / pink curing salt #1): use only at the recommended rate. It’s dyed pink so no one confuses it with table salt.
  • Zatarain’s liquid crab boil: a tiny splash gives that south Louisiana boiled-seafood vibe to pickled pork brine without tasting like straight crab boil.
  • Tony Chachere’s / Slap Ya Mama etc.: many blends are salty. If a recipe also has kosher salt, taste first.
  • Local kalabasa: roast instead of boiling to drive off water and concentrate sweetness. Watery puree = bland pie.
